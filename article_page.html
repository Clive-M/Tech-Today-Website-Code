Business

Cisco, a leading networking hardware maker, experienced a significant dip in its shares, plummeting as much as 13% in extended trading on Wednesday. This drop followed the company's issuance of a gloomy forecast for both the current quarter and the full fiscal year. In terms of financial performance, Cisco reported earnings of $1.11 per share (adjusted), surpassing analysts' expectations of $1.03 per share. The revenue for the quarter was $14.67 billion, slightly above the expected $14.61 billion. The fiscal first quarter saw a 7.6% increase in revenue, reaching $14.67 billion. Net income also rose to $3.64 billion, or 89 cents per share, compared to $2.67 billion, or 65 cents per share, in the same quarter last year. Cisco attributed the slowdown in new product orders during the quarter to clients focusing on installing and implementing products after a robust delivery in the previous three quarters. Cisco CEO Chuck Robbins acknowledged this challenge on a conference call with analysts, emphasizing that customers and sales organizations had clearly communicated this issue over the past 90 days. However, he noted that sales cycles remained longer than usual. The company anticipates that one or two quarters of shipped products are awaiting implementation. As for guidance, Cisco provided a projection of 82 cents to 84 cents in adjusted earnings per share on $12.6 billion to $12.8 billion in revenue for the fiscal second quarter, indicating a 6.6% revenue decline. Analysts had initially expected 99 cents in adjusted earnings per share on $14.19 billion. Despite reducing its full-year revenue forecast, Cisco increased its outlook for earnings. The revised figures stand at $3.87 to $3.93 in adjusted earnings per share on $53.8 billion to $55.0 billion in revenue. In August, the company had forecasted $3.19 to $3.32 in adjusted earnings per share and $57.0 billion to $58.2 billion in revenue. Analysts surveyed by Refinitiv had anticipated $4.05 in adjusted earnings per share and revenue of $57.76 billion. During the quarter, Cisco made headlines with its announcement of plans to acquire data analytics software maker Splunk for a staggering $28 billion. Looking ahead, Cisco remains optimistic about its prospects in the artificial intelligence (AI) infrastructure market. CEO Chuck Robbins expressed confidence in winning over $1 billion worth of orders for AI infrastructure from cloud providers in the 2025 fiscal year. Cisco has adopted a flexible approach when collaborating with cloud providers, enabling it to regain footing in the competitive landscape. Robbins emphasized the importance of a standard, broad-based technology like Ethernet for supporting AI infrastructure. Despite the after-hours setback, Cisco's shares have seen a 12% increase year-to-date, trailing behind the S&P 500 index, which has risen by 17% over the same period. The challenges posed by longer sales cycles and a slowdown in product orders indicate a complex landscape for Cisco as it navigates the evolving market conditions.

Automation

Introduction: Wining and dining, creative offers, and big bonuses may provide the glamor to sales work, but drafting responses to tedious requests for proposals (RFPs) has long been considered a necessary drudgery. However, the landscape of sales administration is undergoing a transformative shift with the introduction of generative AI tools. Companies like Twilio are leveraging advanced technologies such as OpenAI's GPT-4 to automate the process of responding to RFPs, liberating sales teams from hours of mundane work. The Rise of RFP Genie: In April, Twilio unveiled RFP Genie, a generative AI tool designed to streamline the response process to RFPs. This tool digests the RFP, searches internal files for relevant information, and employs GPT-4 to generate a suitable response. What once took a team of staffers two weeks or more can now be accomplished in minutes. Twilio's initiative signals a significant shift in how businesses approach RFPs, embracing the efficiency and accuracy that AI can bring to the table. Industry-Wide Adoption: Twilio is not alone in this endeavor. Google's cloud unit, ad-buying agency EssenceMediacom, DataRobot, and even consulting giants like Bain and Deloitte have incorporated generative AI RFP response bots into their sales processes. IBM, in its internal AI hackathon, selected an RFP bot as the winner, highlighting the potential of AI in revolutionizing traditional business practices. The Birth of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG): Twilio's approach to RFP automation involves what the industry now calls Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). This methodology combines a program that retrieves relevant information from internal sources with GPT-4, resulting in highly accurate responses. RAG minimizes the risk of AI-generated content errors and serves as a cost-effective alternative to training large language models for specific tasks. Efficiency Without Job Cuts: Contrary to fears of job displacement, companies like Twilio emphasize that AI is not replacing human roles but rather enhancing productivity. Twilio's RFP Genie allows its sales teams to focus on more complex issues that demand human contextualization, thereby improving overall sales pitches and customer interactions. The Human Touch: While AI-generated responses are efficient, human oversight remains crucial. Twilio and other companies ensure that solutions engineers and technical experts review or edit AI-generated answers before sending them to clients. This collaboration between AI and human expertise ensures accuracy and maintains the personal touch necessary for successful sales. The Future of RFP Automation: As AI-powered RFP bots continue to gain acceptance, the industry is likely to witness further automation in related processes. The possibility of AI-generated questions, scoring proposals, and recommending winners raises questions about the future role of humans in business admin practices. The AI RFP is seen as a starting point for a more efficient and streamlined approach to the entire RFP process. Conclusion: The integration of generative AI into the realm of RFP responses marks a significant leap forward in sales efficiency. As companies embrace these technologies, the collaborative efforts of AI and human expertise promise not only time savings but also improved sales pitches and customer interactions. The AI RFP is not a threat to jobs but a tool that empowers sales teams to tackle more complex challenges, ultimately reshaping the landscape of white-collar work in the corporate world.
